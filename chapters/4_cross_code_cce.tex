\section{Introduction}

Being able to compare gravitational waveforms with each other, whether it be
comparing Numerical Relativity (NR) waveforms with other NR waveforms or
observed gravitational waves (GWs), is an extremely important ability in GW
science; potentially the most important. GW detectors compare suspected signals
to models that have been informed by NR such as the IMRPhenom family
(examples: \cite{Pratten:2020fqn, Garcia-Quiros:2020qpx, Pratten:2020ceb}) for
frequency-domain analysis and the SEOBNR family (examples:
\cite{Khalil:2023kep, Ramos-Buades:2023ehm, vandeMeent:2023ols}) for time-domain
analysis. NR codes also compare waveforms internally, typically for verification
of convergence as in \S\ref{sec:gws} or for analysis of trends in GW catalogs
\cite{Scheel:2025jct}.

However, the coordinate freedom of GR makes this a complicated task. Comparing
waveforms from different NR simulations, even from the same code, has to be done
carefully since different simulations will be done in different gauges, and
therefore many quantities that depend on the gauge can't be directly compared.

\subsection{Electricity and Magnetism example}

To give an example of why we need to consider gauges when comparing NR
waveforms, let us first look at a more familiar use of gauges in an introductory
setting such as electricity and magnetism to gain intuition. Maxwell's equations
(in vector form) are
\begin{align}
  \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = 4\pi\rho &\quad\quad
  \nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{1}{c}\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t} \\
  \nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0 &\quad\quad
  \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \frac{1}{c}\left(4\pi\mathbf{J} + \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\right)
\end{align}
From Gauss's law for magnetism and Faraday's equation along with vector calculus
identities, we can define
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{B} &= \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\label{eq:maxwell-b} \\
  \mathbf{E} &= -\nabla\phi - \frac{\partial \mathbf{A}}{\partial t}\label{eq:maxwell-e}
\end{align}
where $\phi$ is the electric potential and $\mathbf{A}$ is the vector potential.
We notice that if we modify $\phi$ and $\mathbf{A}$ like so
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{A} &\rightarrow \mathbf{A} + \nabla\psi\label{eq:maxwell-a} \\
  \phi &\rightarrow \phi - \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t}\label{eq:maxwell-phi}
\end{align}
for some scalar function $\psi$, $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{E}$ in
Eqs. \ref{eq:maxwell-b} and \ref{eq:maxwell-e}, respectively, remain unchanged. This
ability to choose $\phi$ and $\mathbf{A}$ is called gauge freedom, and the
choice that we make is considered the gauge.

In electricity and magnetism there are two common gauges; Coulomb and Lorenz.
The Coulomb gauge is defined by
\begin{equation}
  \nabla \cdot \mathbf{A} = 0
\end{equation}
To describe the Lorenz gauge, we combine $\phi$ and $\mathbf{A}$ as a
four-vector
\begin{equation}
  A^\mu = \left(\phi/c,\mathbf{A}\right) .
\end{equation}
The Lorenz gauge is then
\begin{equation}
  \partial_\mu A^\mu = 0.
\end{equation}
This differs from the Coulomb gauge because of the $\partial_0 A^0 \sim
\partial_t \phi$ part. The implications that this choice of gauge has on the
equations can be found in virtually any electricity and magnetism textbook
\cite{Jackson:1998nia,Griffiths:2017ngp}. The important point for this discussion is that even though
$\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ mathematically remain unchanged depending on which
gauge is chosen, the gauge dependent quantities $\phi$ and $\mathbf{A}$ cannot
be compared between gauges.

\subsection{General and Numerical Relativity gauges}

Now we move to gauges in general relativity (GR). Instead of putting a
constraint on the four-potential as our gauge condition, in GR we impose our
gauge condition on the coordinates $x^\mu$ of our spacetime. This coordinate
freedom is briefly explored in Ch. \ref{ch:intro}, but a full derivation can be
found in virtually any GR text book, including
\cite{Misner:1973prb,Wald:1984rg,Carroll:2004st} to name a few. For our
purposes, let's take a look at a few gauges in GR and NR.

\subsubsection{Transverse-traceless gauge}
The first gauge most students encounter is one called the transverse-traceless
(TT) gauge, occasionally called the radiation gauge. The TT gauge is
particularly useful in a linearized theory of gravity where we define our metric
as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear_gravity}
  g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + \bar{h}_{\mu\nu}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  \bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = h_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}\eta_{\mu\nu}h
\end{equation}
where $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ is our flat background spacetime metric and $h_{\mu\nu}$
is a small perturbation of this background. Here, $\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ is a
``trace-reversed'' form. We take our field equations
\begin{equation}\label{eq:field_equations}
  R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu} 
\end{equation}
and substitute Eq. \ref{eq:linear_gravity} into them. This will contain second
derivatives of $\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ because of the Ricci tensor and scalar. It is at
this point where we make a gauge choice of
\begin{equation}\label{eq:harmonic_gauge}
  \Box x^\mu = 0
\end{equation}
which can be shown to simplify to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:linear_harmonic_gauge}
  \Box x^\mu = 0 \rightarrow g^{\mu\nu}\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu} = 0 \rightarrow
  \partial_\mu {\bar{h}^\mu}_{\lambda} = 0
\end{equation}
in the weak-field (linear) limit\cite{Carroll:2004st}. Plugging Eq. \ref{eq:linear_harmonic_gauge}
into Eq. \ref{eq:field_equations} and taking the vacuum case ($R_{\mu\nu} =
T_{\mu\nu} = 0$), we arrive at a clean result
\begin{equation}
  \Box \bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0 .
\end{equation}
It can be shown (through some math that is unimportant to the point here)
\cite{Carroll:2004st} that
\begin{equation}
  h^{\textrm{TT}}_{\mu\nu} = \left(
\begin{matrix}
  0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
  0 & h_{+} & h_{\times} & 0 \\
  0 & h_{\times} & -h_{+} & 0 \\
  0 & 0 & 0 & 0
\end{matrix}
\right) ,
\end{equation}
where we have dropped the bars because $h_{\mu\nu}$ is the trace-reverse of
$\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ which is traceless. These are the familiar GW polarizations
$h_{+}$ and $h_{\times}$ for a linear GW propagating in the z-direction.
Therefore, this is a useful gauge to analyze GWs from one of the detectors
(though it's quite a bit more complicated than this in reality
\cite{KAGRA:2021vkt,LIGOScientific:2021usb,LIGOScientific:2020ibl,LIGOScientific:2018mvr}).

\subsubsection{NR gauges}

Moving on to NR, the two most popular formulations of the field equations (Eq.
\ref{eq:field_equations}) are the Generalized Harmonic (GH) formulation
\cite{Garfinkle:2001ni,Lindblom:2005qh} and the BSSN/Z4 family
\cite{Nakamura:1987zz,Shibata:1995we,Baumgarte:1998te,Bona:2003fj,Bernuzzi:2009ex,Hilditch:2012fp,Alic:2011gg}.
Each of these
formulations use different gauge conditions for the evolution of the coordinates
$x^\mu$, none that are equal to the TT gauge.

We start with GH. There are two dominant gauges used when evolving the GH
formulation. The first is actually the harmonic gauge introduced in Eq.
\ref{eq:harmonic_gauge}. The TT gauge is actually a sub-category of the harmonic
gauge, useful for linear theories. However, GH is the fully non-linear theory of
gravity and so the TT gauge is not suitable here. The harmonic gauge is
convenient because the solutions for the equations of the coordinates are
harmonic (or wave) solutions, hence the name, and we know how to solve for
waves.

The second gauge commonly used in GH evolutions is one that actually builds off
of the harmonic gauge, because its wave solutions are so desirable. The general
form of this gauge, called the damped-harmonic gauge, is
\begin{equation}
  \Box x_\mu = H_\mu(x^\nu, g_{\nu\kappa})
\end{equation}
where $H_\mu$ is called the gauge-source function~\cite{Szilagyi:2009qz}, and it
is a function of the coordinates and the spacetime metric. The gauge source
function is defined in Eq. \ref{eq:dh_gauge_source}--\ref{eq:dh_mu3}. The main
purpose for this is to damp away oscillations in the shift $\alpha$ and drive it
to one (so the time evolution remains steady).

For the BSSN/Z4 family of formulations, a gauge condition called the
moving-puncture gauge, combined with $1+\log$ slicing and a
$\Gamma$-driver condition, is used. For this family, conditions are put onto the
lapse function $\alpha$ and the shift vector $\beta^i$ instead of having
equations for the coordinates themselves. The $1+\log$ slicing condition is (Ch.
4 of \cite{Baumgarte:2010ndz})
\begin{equation}\label{eq:1_plus_log}
  \partial_t \alpha = -2\alpha K + \beta^j\partial_j\alpha
\end{equation}
where $K$ is the extrinsic curvature. The $\Gamma$-driver condition can come
in two forms (Ch. 4 of \cite{Baumgarte:2010ndz})
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma_driver_1}
  \partial_t \beta^i = \eta B^i + \beta^j\partial_j\beta^i,
\end{equation}
or (\cite{Hilditch:2012fp})
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma_driver_2}
  \partial_t \beta^i = \tilde{\Gamma}^i - \eta\beta^i + \beta^j\partial_j\beta^i,
\end{equation}
where $\eta$ is a tunable parameter, and $\tilde{\Gamma}^i$ is related to the
Christoffel symbols. The former is typically used with BSSN while the latter is
used with the Z4 family, but many NR codes make slight modifications to any of
these gauge equations.

It should be fairly clear that trying to compare a GW produced with the GH
formulation to a GW produced with the BSSN formulation is not well-defined
because the gauges that are used are completely different. However, more subtly,
comparing GWs that were produced with the same formulation is also ill-defined.
This is due to the limitations of numerical resolution and floating-point
arithmetic when solving Einstein's Equations on the computer. Because of these
numerical issues, even simulations that start with the same initial data but are
evolved with different spatial or temporal resolutions may not evolve in an
identical manner. Therefore, the coordinates of these different resolution
simulations will be different at the same ``time'' of the simulation. This can
be seen in the bottom two panels of Figure \ref{fig:BbhGWs} where we are
comparing the phase and amplitude difference of the GWs from different
resolutions of BBH simulations evolved with the same initial data.

\section{Cauchy-Characteristic Extraction}

Now that we have detailed a number of different useful gauges, namely the TT,
harmonic, damped harmonic, and moving-puncture gauges, it should be clear that
the GW strains $h$ we compute from simulations done in these different gauges
cannot directly be compared with each other. The coordinate freedom of GR
implies that even if we use the same formulas to compute $h$, we will get
different answers because the coordinates are completely different. Therefore,
how can we compare waveforms across codes? The answer is, by comparing the
waveforms at a location where our choice of coordinates mostly don't matter;
Namely at future-null infinity, also denoted \scri. Future-null infinity is the
location in spacetime that future null rays (e.g. light rays) escape if they
don't terminate inside the black hole and aren't in a stable orbit.

There are a few ways of computing GWs at \scri~that allow for comparison of GWs
between resolutions or between codes. Most NR codes don't have a computational
domain that extends out to \scri, as this would be intractably computationally
expensive with typical methods. Instead, GWs at \scri~must be computed in
another manner. The first, and by far most inaccurate, method is to simply
ignore gauge effects and compare GWs that are extracted at finite radii within
the computational domain, treating them as if they were at \scri. While this
method cannot be used to compare waveforms between NR codes, when comparing
similar simulations from the same code, it can be a quick-and-dirty method for a
rapid analysis.

The second, and by far most common, method for computing GWs at \scri~is called
extrapolation. The details for extrapolation can be found in the SXS
Collaboration's second catalog release \cite{Boyle:2019kee}. However, the overall
method is to compute the $\Psi_4$ Weyl scalar and the strain $h$, either
directly using Regge-Wheeler-Zerilli methods~\cite{Sarbach:2001PhysRevD,Regge:1957PhysRev,Zerilli:1970PhysRevLett} or taking two time integrals of $\Psi_4$, on a number of
concentric spheres surrounding the origin of the coordinate system. These
quantities are then extrapolated with a $1/r^N$ power law, where $N$ is chosen
based on a number of different conditions described in \cite{Boyle:2019kee}.

Extrapolation has been very successful and has been widely used by the NR
community for producing GW catalogs
\cite{Boyle:2019kee,Healy:2022wdn,Ferguson:2023vta,Hamilton:2023qkv}. However,
because it simply extrapolates waveforms from the near field region around the
black hole, some non-linear effects of GR as the wave travels to \scri~are not
accounted for and may be important. Even with memory corrected waveforms from
\cite{Scheel:2025jct}, they still don't account for all non-linear effects. For
this, a full evolution of Einstein's equations out to \scri~is needed.

\subsection{Characteristic Evolution}

A method to evolve Einstein's equations all the way out to \scri~is called
Cauchy-Characteristic Extraction/Evolution (CCE). Here, we detail the important
concepts and equations of CCE that will be needed in the rest of the chapter,
following \cite{Moxon:2020gha} and \cite{Moxon:2021gbv}.

CCE works by first performing a Cauchy evolution of the spacetime using one of
the formulations we discussed earlier this chapter (GH, BSSN, Z4). Then, we
store the spacetime metric and its derivatives on a sphere centered at the
origin for all times during our simulation. This is called the worldtube. This
worldtube data is then used as the inner boundary condition for the
Characteristic evolution which is performed on a domain starting at the
worldtube and extending out to \scri, represented as compactified coordinates.
This characteristic evolution is performed on null-hypersurfaces, unlike the
Cauchy evolution which is performed on space-like hypersurfaces. This is because
with the Characteristic evolution, we are primarily concerned with evolving and
resolving GWs, which travel along null rays, making null-hypersurfaces well
suited to this problem.

CCE decomposes the metric into yet another set of coordinates and gauge
conditions, distinct from those used in GH or BSSN. The metric in a Bondi-like
spherical coordinate system $\{u,r,x^A\}$ is
\begin{align}
  ds^2 &= -\left(e^{2\beta}\frac{V}{r} - r^2h_{AB}U^AU^B\right)du^2 - 2e^{2\beta}dudr \nonumber\\
    &\quad\quad -2r^2h_{AB}U^Bdudx^A + r^2h_{AB}dx^Adx^B
\end{align}
where $h_{AB}$ is related to the metric on the 2-sphere $q_{AB}$, $V$ and
$\beta$ are scalar functions, and $U^A$ is a vector on the 2-sphere. Note that
$\beta$ should not be confused with the shift vector $\beta^i$. These Bondi-like
coordinates have the gauge conditions that $g_{rr} = g_{rA} = 0$,
$\det{(h_{AB})} = \det{(q_{AB})}$, and asymptotic behavior
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{x\rightarrow\infty} \{\beta, U^A, h_{AB}, \frac{V-r}{r^2} \} = \mathcal{O}(r^0) .
\end{equation}
We can define a complex dyad $q^A$ (basis vector) of $q_{AB}$ as the following
\begin{equation}
  q_{AB} = \frac{1}{2}\left(q_A\bar{q}_B + \bar{q}_Aq_B\right),
\end{equation}
with barred quantities being the complex conjugate. Additionally, if a quantity
transforms like
\begin{equation}
  q_A \rightarrow q_A e^{is\psi}
\end{equation}
for some phase $\psi$, then we say that quantity has spin weight $s$. With these
definitions, it is now convenient to define some scalars that will be important
for the CCE evolution:
\begin{align}
  \label{eq:bondi_u}
  U &\equiv U^Aq_A & (s=1) \\
  Q &\equiv r^2e^{-2\beta}q^Ah_{AB}\partial_r U^B & (s=1) \\
  r^2W &\equiv V-r & (s=0) \\
  J &\equiv \frac{1}{2}q^Aq^Bh_{AB} & (s=2) \\
  K &\equiv \sqrt{1+J\bar{J}} & (s=0) \\
  \label{eq:bondi_h}
  H &\equiv \partial_u J & (s=2)
\end{align}

Now we can define a hierarchical set of evolution equations for these scalars
which will give us the characteristic evolution of the spacetime on null-slices.
The first of these equations that is needed is actually Eq. \ref{eq:bondi_h},
which will give us $J$. Then once we have $J$, we can build up a solution by
solving radial differential equations for the other scalars. Here we will denote
the right hand side of each equation with a generic function $S$ because the
details of what is on the right hand side are not important. See
\cite{Moxon:2020gha} again for the full equations.
\begin{align}
   \partial_r\beta &= S_\beta(J), \\
   \partial_r (r^2Q) &= S_Q(J,\beta), \\
   \partial_r U &= S_U(J,\beta,Q), \\
   \partial_r (r^2W) &= S_W(J,\beta,Q,U), \\
   \partial_r (rH) + L_H(J,\beta,Q,U,W)H &\nonumber\\
   + L_{\bar{H}}(J,\beta,Q,U,W)\bar{H} &= S_H(J,\beta,Q,U,W)
\end{align}
The final radial equation for $H$ lets us solve Eq. \ref{eq:bondi_h} for J at
the next time step and the hierarchy starts over again.

Now these scalars are defined in terms of our metric expressed in a Bondi-like
gauge which is useful for evolving the Characteristic system. However, in order
for us to impose boundary conditions with worldtube data from a Cauchy evolution
performed in some arbitrary evolved gauge, we
must transform the evolved gauge into our Bondi-like gauge. The mathematics of
this transformation are quite tedious and unimportant for the point being made
here because it uses a number of intermediate coordinates that are only helpful
for the transformation, so we will skip them. To see them in detail, refer to
\cite{Moxon:2020gha}. Instead we just state that our scalars defined in
\ref{eq:bondi_u}-\ref{eq:bondi_h} can be expressed as some function of the
Cauchy spacetime metric and Cauchy evolved coordinates (e.g. $U =
f(x^\mu,g_{\mu\nu})$, where $x^\mu$ is not related to $x^A$), with all the
coordinate transformations hidden.

\subsection{Frame fixing and BMS transformations}
Now that we have a way to evolve GWs out to \scri, unfortunately, we still
aren't finished. While we could directly compare GWs evolved from CCE, we
actually still have some coordinate freedom left to exploit, specifically
because we are at \scri. This coordinate freedom is the Bondi-van der
Burg-Metzner-Sachs (BMS) group of symmetries, which is the standard Lorentz
group plus an infinite dimensional group called supertranslations
\cite{Bondi:1962px,Sachs:1962wk}. A supertranslation is an angle-dependent time
translation where every point on the 2-sphere of \scri~can be time translated
independently of each other because they are all causally disconnected. With
this freedom, we can take two NR waveforms and map them to a common BMS frame
for comparison. The most common frames used are the post-Newtonian (PN) BMS
frame (i.e. the frame that PN waveforms are in), the inspiral superrest frame
(i.e. the frame of the inspiraling BHs), and the remnant superrest frame (i.e.
the frame of the remnant black hole after a merger) \cite{Mitman:2022kwt}. The
transformation of CCE waveforms into one of these frames is called frame fixing.

The math behind frame fixing to the PN BMS or superrest frame is particularly
tedious. However, all calculations are provided by the Python code \texttt{scri}
\cite{mike_boyle_2020_4041972}, which accepts the output from \spectre~CCE.
Therefore, in order to compare waveforms one must not only perform
characteristic evolutions with \spectre~CCE, but one must also perform frame
fixing on the CCE output of two waveforms being compared to put them into the
same BMS frame. While this final frame fixing is crucial for being able to
compare CCE waveforms, we only note it here and refer the reader to the sources
cited in this section for further details as they are not important to the
transformation of Cauchy worldtube data described below.

\section{Worldtube data formats}
Now we have the basics of the Characteristic evolution. Specifically that we use
the Bondi scalars defined in Eqs. \ref{eq:bondi_u}-\ref{eq:bondi_h} as part of a
hierarchical evolution system, we need those Bondi scalars on the worldtube
which serve as the inner boundary condition for the Characteristic evolution,
and these Bondi scalars on the worldtube can be computed from the Cauchy metric,
coordinates, and the spatial and temporal derivatives of the metric. Next we
focus on how this last step is implemented in \spectre~and what new features
were added in \spectre.

\subsection{Original worldtube data format}
The first detail we need to discuss is how \spectre~actually represents and
stores the Bondi scalars either in memory of the simulation or on disk. Since we
need the Bondi scalars on a sphere of constant radius at each timestep,
representing the scalars as complex spherical harmonic coefficients is the most
natural. For a surface $S_{\ell,m}$, we require $m=\ell$. This implies we will
use $M = (\ell+1)^2$ complex-valued coefficients (or modes) to represent each
Bondi scalar. We will call this the ``modal'' representation. The modal
representation is extremely useful for two reasons. First is that it is straightforward
to convert between two representations with different $\ell$ since
the conversion is either truncating the number of coefficients or appending
coefficients with value zero. Second, is that the modal representation is
significantly more compact than a ``nodal'' representation where we represent
the complex valued scalar at collocation points on a sphere. For the same
surface $S_{\ell,m}$ the number of nodes needed to represent the scalar is $N =
(\ell+1)(2\ell+1) > M$. The advantage with using a nodal representation for the
actual Characteristic evolution is that we can easily perform arithmetic between
Bondi scalars which is needed for the spectral algorithms that \spectre~uses.
This is significantly more difficult with a modal representation because you
cannot perform arithmetic with coefficients.

We now have a situation where it is ideal to represent the evolved Bondi
scalars with nodal values, but the worldtube Bondi variables with modal
coefficients. The worldtube variables must be written to disk during the Cauchy
evolution, so we want those to take up as little disk space as possible. Also,
there's no guarantee that the $\ell$ we write the worldtube variables with will
be the same $\ell$ that we use for the inner boundary of our Characteristic
evolution. One may even want to run multiple Characteristic evolutions with
varying resolution.

The \spectre~CCE algorithm \cite{Moxon:2021gbv} was previously designed to
accept two different forms of the Bondi variables on the worldtube. The first is
reading in $\alpha$, $\beta^i$, $g_{ij}$, and their derivatives each written out
in complex modal form. This was to support a legacy output format from the
\spec~code \cite{Taylor:2013zia}. The second is directly reading in the Bondi
scalars in complex modal form in the Cauchy evolution gauge. This format is
output from the modern \spec~simulations \cite{Scheel:2025jct}, and from all
\spectre~simulations \cite{deppe_2025_16906840}. Both \spec~and \spectre~have
routines within the Cauchy evolution that compute the necessary Bondi scalars in
the evolution gauge on the worldtube. The \spectre~CCE algorithm then reads in
both these data formats. For the metric variables, a conversion to Bondi scalars
is first computed within the CCE algorithm.

\subsection{New worldtube data formats}

In this work, we have added three more input formats for \spectre~CCE. They are
1) Bondi scalars in complex nodal format, 2) metric variables in complex modal
format but without needing the time derivatives of the metric quantities, and 3)
metric variables in complex nodal format, both with and without the time
derivatives of the metric quantities. We have added support for reading in nodal
data formats because it is often easier for other NR codes besides \spec~and
\spectre~to output data onto a set of grid points rather than transforming the
data to the exact form of complex spherical harmonic coefficients that
\spectre~requires. Our spherical harmonic formulation, however, does require the
worldtube data to be output at specific grid, or collocation, points necessary
for the CCE algorithm. To aid in giving the correct collocation points,
\spectre~offers a separate executable that will write the correct collocation
points for a given radius to a text file in the necessary order. \spectre~also
offers a C routine that can be added to the source code that will give an array
of the necessary collocation points.

We have expanded support for reading in metric quantities for the worldtube
because most other NR codes use either the BSSN or Z4 formulations of Einstein's
equations. Particularly, we have relaxed the requirement to provide time
derivatives of the metric quantities for the worldtube data. This was done
because unlike the GH formulation where one of the evolved variables, $\Pi$,
\textit{is} the time derivative of the spacetime metric, the BSSN and Z4
formulations only evolve the metric itself. Even though the time derivative of
the metric quantities are computed for the Cauchy evolution, they are often not
available for output. The time derivatives within the evolution would be
out-of-sync with the actual evolved metric quantities by one time step and
trying to sync up the data is a much larger change for other codes.

Therefore, we allow for computing the time derivatives internally in
\spectre~CCE. For the spatial metric we use the 3+1 equation
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t\gamma_{ij} = -2\alpha K_{ij} + D_i\beta_j + D_j\beta_i .
\end{equation}
Extreme care must be taken so that we use the
correct gauge equations for $\alpha$ and $\beta^i$. Luckily for $\alpha$, most
other codes use the $1+\log$ slicing condition given by Eq. \ref{eq:1_plus_log}.
Some codes choose to not include the advective term $\beta^j\partial_j\alpha$,
so we have added it as optional. For the gamma-driver condition, we support both
Eqs. \ref{eq:gamma_driver_1} and \ref{eq:gamma_driver_2}. Again, we make the
advective term $\beta^j\partial_j\beta^i$ optional. For the different
gamma-driver conditions, they require different variables to be written to disk
from the Cauchy evolution. This is the responsibility of the user to know which
variables need to be written out.

Due to the increased number of supported worldtube data formats, the inputs to
the actual \spectre~CCE algorithm have been simplified. We now restrict the
worldtube input data format to only be the Bondi variables represented in
complex modal coefficients. This was done largely to avoid waiting for extra
computations during the Characteristic evolution for the worldtube data. It was
also done to simplify the code-paths taken during the Characteristic evolution.
Therefore, in order to support all these worldtube data formats, \spectre~now
offers a separate executable that will transform the worldtube data from any of
the supported input formats to the Bondi scalars in complex modal form in a
separate output file. The new output file is then fed directly into the CCE
algorithm. The new executable has the ability to handle overlapping output times
and multiple output files from different segments of a simulation.

Finally, in order to make these new improvements available to the NR community,
\spectre~now offers a
\href{https://github.com/sxs-collaboration/spectre/releases}{downloadable
\texttt{tar} file} which has the following pre-built executables:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The CCE executable itself plus an associated input file.
  \item A preprocessing executable to convert the worldtube data into the Bondi
        modal format plus associated input files with examples for each of
        the accepted input data types.
  \item An executable to write the collocation points for a sphere at a given
        radius and angular resolution $\ell$ to interpolate input data to.
  \item Testing scripts plus a small amount of example worldtube data used to
        verify the executables work.
\end{enumerate}
Additionally, we have added detailed documentation to the
\href{https://spectre-code.org/index.html}{\spectre~webpage} with instructions
for how to use these executables, recommended settings for certain input
parameters, and other small pieces of knowledge that were empirically determined
while running CCE.

\section{Examples of NR codes using \spectre~CCE\label{sec:cce_codes_examples}}
Now that we have detailed the process for converting Cauchy worldtube data into the
proper format for \spectre~CCE, we present some examples where this is
being used.

\subsection{\spectre\label{sec:cce_spectre}}

The first example is from \S\ref{sec:gws}. Here we are comparing waveforms from
different resolutions of a GH BBH simulation with mass ratio $q=1$ and
non-spinning black holes. Since these simulations were run with \spectre, the
worldtube data is in the Bondi modal format directly accepted by CCE. The goal
of comparing the different resolutions is to assess the convergence of
\spectre~BBH simulations. Therefore, for that study, we did not actually
transform the different resolutions to a common superrest frame for comparison.
Instead, we transformed each resolution separately to the superrest frame of the
inspiral of that resolution (i.e. a frame where the binary is at rest during
early inspiral). Doing this allows for a convergence test because each
resolution is transformed into a superrest frame without information from the
other resolutions, effectively keeping each resolution isolated from the others.

\begin{figure}
  \hfill
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{images/cross_code_cce/spectre_inspiral_superrest_comparison.pdf}
  \caption[\spectre~GW modes mapped to the inspiral superrest frame at three resolutions.]{\spectre~GWs mapped to the inspiral superrest frame of the ``Lev2'' simulation
    at time $t-t_{22}^\mathrm{peak}=-3000M$ before the peak of the amplitude of
    the $2,2$ mode of the waveform. All waveforms are plotted so $t=0$
    corresponds to this peak of the $2,2$ mode. The four rows are the
    $\ell,m=(2,2),(2,0),(3,2),(4,4)$ modes of the waveforms, respectively. The left
    column is the amplitude of each mode. The middle column is the fractional
    amplitude difference with respect to one higher Lev. The third column is the
    phase difference of the waveforms where we zero out the phase of each
    waveform at $t-t_{22}^{\mathrm{peak}} = -4000M$, consistent with Fig.
    \ref{fig:BbhGWs}. Since the $\ell,m=(2,0)$ isn't oscillatory (i.e. real), we
    omit the phase difference plot for this mode. \label{fig:BbhGWsLev2Superrest}}
\end{figure}

In Fig. \ref{fig:BbhGWsLev2Superrest} we choose a common superrest frame,
specifically the inspiral superrest frame of the ``Lev2'' simulation at a time
of $t-t_{22}^\mathrm{peak}=-3000M$, and then map the other two Levs to this
superrest frame. The amplitude and phase differences for the $\ell,m=(2,2)$ mode
are similar to that of Fig. \ref{fig:BbhGWs} and we don't see a noticeable
improvement mapping to the same superrest frame. For the $2,0$ mode, the early
time amplitude differences are likely due to poor initial data on the initial
null slice of the characteristic evolution. The late time behavior of the $2,0$
mode is likely due to the cubed spheres that \spectre~uses in the ringdown
domain. The $3,2$ and $4,4$ modes show similar behavior to the $2,2$, just at a
smaller amplitude.

The next example is running \spectre~CCE over a waveform from the SXS catalog
\cite{Scheel:2025jct}. We chose \texttt{SXS:BBH:2696}, a $q=8$ quasi-circular
BBH with dimensionless spin $\vec{\chi}=(0,0,0.8)$ on each black hole, which was
performed using \spec~and was simulated for 43 orbits. This simulation was also
evolved in the GH gauge and output its data in the Bondi modal format directly
accepted by CCE. In Fig. \ref{fig:spec-bbh-cce}, we show similar plots to Fig.
\ref{fig:BbhGWsLev2Superrest} with the $(2,2)$, $(2,0)$, and $(3,2)$ modes, but
we plot the $(2,1)$ mode instead of the $(4,4)$ mode since this
simulation is asymmetric in its masses, so we expect the $(2,1)$ mode to be
excited.

In Fig. \ref{fig:spec-bbh-cce}, we map each Lev to its own superrest frame at
time $t-t_{22}^\mathrm{peak}=-8694M$ and then compare the waveforms. All the
amplitude and phase comparisons appear to be convergent, except for the
fractional amplitude comparison of the $2,0$ mode. But that only appears
slightly non-convergent. Otherwise, the amplitude of the $2,0$ mode looks
significantly better than \spectre's. Also, the phase differences in Fig.
\ref{fig:spec-bbh-cce} appear to be much better behaved than in \spectre, even
with a waveform that is roughly double its length.

\begin{figure}

Next we summarize results from \cite{Radice:2025djo} which runs \spectre~CCE on
simulations performed with the AthenaK NR code \cite{Zhu:2024utz}. AthenaK evolves the Z4c
formalism of Einstein's equations using the moving punctures gauge with the
$1+\log$ condition and the $\Gamma$-driver condition, both including the
advective terms. In Fig. \ref{fig:athenak-cce} we show an AthenaK waveform run
through CCE and frame fixed to a superrest frame at time just after junk
radiation has dissipated. Here we plot the $\ell,m=(2,2),(2,0),(2,1),(3,2)$
modes of the waveform, again showing that CCE is able to run on worldtube data
from other codes. For a more in-depth analysis of this waveform and GW event
GW150914, see \cite{Radice:2025djo}. In order for AthenaK to compute radial
derivatives, metric data was dumped on a number of concentric spheres at radii
spaced using Chebyshev collocation points \cite{radice_athenak_tutorial}. Radial
derivatives are then computed with Chebyshev polynomials around the middle
spherical shell \cite{radice_athenak_tutorial}.

\spectre~CCE can run on more than just data from a BBH. Any compact merger with
dynamical gravity that can be simulated can supply worldtube data and then be
run through CCE. In Fig. \ref{fig:spec-fil-bns-cce}, we present a case of two

equal mass non-spinning $1.35M_{\odot}$ NSs. Here, we don't provide an analysis
of the waveforms as this work is still in progress \cite{Sun:2026}, but we
simply show this as proof that we can run \spectre~CCE on non-SXS codes.

Finally, we show in-progress work of \spectre~CCE running on worldtube data
produced by the Einstein Toolkit~\cite{Loffler:2011ay,EinsteinToolkit:2025_05}.
The data was output with the \texttt{CCE\_Export}

$R=200M$ used here.

\section{Conclusion}
We present additions and improvements to the \spectre~CCE module that allow for
running CCE on the output of other NR codes besides the SXS Collaboration's
\spec~and \spectre. \spectre~CCE itself now only accepts worldtube data in the
form of the Bondi scalars from Eq. \ref{eq:bondi_u}-\ref{eq:bondi_h} represented
as complex-valued spherical harmonic coefficients/modes. However, now \spectre~
also provides a conversion executable to put worldtube data into this expected
format. This conversion executable accepts the spatial metric, lapse, and shift,
along with their spatial derivatives as inputs, plus parameters for choosing
which gauge conditions the Cauchy evolution were performed with so the
conversion executable can compute the time derivatives itself.

These improvements were made to better facilitate comparisons of numerical
waveforms between NR codes. First, CCE better captures the non-linear effects of
GR compared to extrapolation because the spacetime metric is actually evolved
out to \scri. Second, CCE accounts for the different evolution gauges used by NR
codes, by computing waveforms in the same frame at \scri~(up to BMS
transformations which can be freely chosen). Prior to this work, comparing NR
waveforms consisted of comparing extrapolated waveforms, assuming they are in
the same gauge.

In \S\ref{sec:cce_codes_examples}, we present several examples of codes using
\spectre~CCE to extract GWs, along with preliminary frame-fixing analysis of
some waveforms. We show CCE running with worldtube data from
\spectre~(\S\ref{sec:cce_spectre}), \spec~(\S\ref{sec:cce_spec}), AthenaK
(\S\ref{sec:cce_athenak}), FIL (\S\ref{sec:cce_fil}), and ETK
(\S\ref{sec:cce_etk}). We give examples for frame-fixing analysis for the
\spectre~and \spec~simulations.

There are a couple avenues for future work available using \spectre~CCE. First,
with \spectre~specifically, is improving the Cauchy evolution to remove the
undesirable features in the waveforms modes seen in Fig.
\ref{fig:BbhGWsLev2Superrest}. This is already underway at Caltech, Cornell, and
Cal State Fullerton and will hopefully be ready soon so \spectre~can produce
catalog-ready waveforms.

A second avenue for future work which will benefit all that use \spectre~CCE is
improving the initial data on the first null slice of the characteristic
evolution. The imperfect initial data can be seen in the early-time amplitude of
the $2,2$ mode in both Fig. \ref{fig:BbhGWsLev2Superrest} and
\ref{fig:spec-bbh-cce}. Significant work has already been put into solving this
problem \cite{DaRe:2026}, but still needs to be investigated further.

A third avenue would be performing a highly detailed study of CCE waveforms from
BBH simulations by different NR codes. To do this study, the exact same initial
data should be used by each NR code, and interpolated onto the domains. This
ensures that the physical parameters for the BBH, e.g. mass ratio and spins, are
as close as possible. Gauge dependent parameters such as coordinate separation,
angular velocity, etc may be different though. Then, each NR code evolves the
BBH through merger to a sufficient time after the BH has rung down. Ideally the
resolution of each simulation from the different codes should be roughly the
same based on some gauge-independent measure. Then all codes use \spectre~CCE to
extract the GWs at \scri. From here, a number of different frame-fixing
techniques can be used to analyze the data such as mapping to the remnant
superrest frame, mapping each code's waveform to their own inspiral superrest
frame, or mapping each code's waveform to one specific code's inspiral superrest
frame. Once the frame fixing is complete, a full analysis of the memory can be
done.

Overall, we believe these improvements to the \spectre~CCE module will benefit
the NR community by facilitating more comparison studies amongst the different
NR codes and groups. This will drive the accuracy of NR waveforms closer to the
necessary requirements for future GW detectors.
